================================================================
TASK: PROBE 3 SUGGESTION BANK GENERATION (multimodal video input)
================================================================

You are given an entire short video (typically 15 to 90 seconds) that a
blind or low-vision creator has filmed and wants to edit. Unlike the
description-generation task, you have access to the full video stream
(visual + audio + temporal motion), not just a single keyframe per
segment. Use that to spot things a single frame cannot show:
continuity, pacing, audio glitches, motion problems, repetition,
moments that change.

Your job is to produce a small bank of 6 to 8 observations the creator
could act on during editing.

================================================================
HOW THIS BANK WILL BE USED
================================================================

This bank is for the third condition of a research probe study. During
the session a researcher will deploy the suggestions one at a time to
the creator. The creator does not see all suggestions at once, and
they cannot apply any suggestion directly. For each one, they pick
one of four routes:

  - I'll Do It (route to themselves)
  - Ask AI to Fix (route to the AI)
  - Ask [helper name] to fix (route to their sighted helper)
  - Dismiss

Therefore each suggestion must:

  1. Stand alone without context from the other suggestions.
  2. Read naturally regardless of which of the four routes is chosen.
  3. Describe an observation the creator can decide what to do with —
     not a directive instruction. ("Scene 4 has a phone in the
     foreground that obstructs the stage" reads well with all four
     routes; "Trim scene 4 to remove the foreground obstruction" only
     reads well with two of them.)

================================================================
THREE CATEGORIES (you must produce at least one of each)
================================================================

ISSUE FLAGS — technical problems the creator probably wants to fix.
  Examples: motion blur on the subject, persistent over- or
  underexposure, focus pull on the wrong subject, foreground
  obstructions, distracting audio glitches, jarring jump cuts that
  appear unintentional, sustained handheld shake.
  Reserve this category for things that are objectively wrong, not
  things that are merely stylistic choices.

STRUCTURAL — observations about how the video is organised as a
whole. The temporal-aware view is the point of this category.
  Examples: a single shot that runs almost unchanged for the entire
  clip and might be trimmed; the most visually distinct moment
  arrives last and might be moved earlier; two segments cover the
  same content; the opening takes longer than necessary to establish
  the scene.

CREATIVE — aesthetic observations the creator might want to consider.
  Examples: the framing stays at one distance throughout (variety),
  no clear opening or closing beat, opportunities for an inserted
  cutaway, lighting changes that could be emphasised or smoothed.
  Frame these as openings to consider, not problems.

================================================================
WRITING RULES
================================================================

1. Each suggestion is one sentence, 12 to 30 words. Concise.

2. Tie each suggestion to a specific scene index (zero-based) using
   `relatedScene`. Use an array of indices when the observation spans
   multiple segments (e.g. "Scenes 3 and 4 cover the same...").
   REQUIRED: `relatedScene` must reference at least one valid index
   from the segments list. Do NOT default every suggestion to
   `relatedScene: 0` — that means you have ignored the segmentation.
   If the segments list is missing or empty, return
   {"error": "segments list required"} and produce no suggestions.

3. Be observational, not directive. Lead with what is visible or
   audible; let the creator decide the action.
   GOOD: "Scene 1 has visible motion blur on the foreground figure
   that obscures the stage."
   BAD: "Trim scene 1 because of motion blur."

   Imply an action without naming one. Every suggestion must point at
   at least one editorial opening the creator could take (trim,
   reorder, add cutaway, add caption, change framing, smooth a
   transition, etc.) without prescribing the specific action. Pure
   state-of-the-shot observations with no implied direction are too
   inert and waste a bank slot.
   INERT: "The framing remains a wide shot, with no close-ups."
   LIVE: "Every scene is framed at the same wide distance from one
   camera position; varied framing or a cutaway could improve visual
   rhythm."

4. Stay factual. Do not invent text on screen, brand names, song
   titles, lyrics, or activity types. If a piece of text is unclear,
   describe it as unclear; do not transcribe a guess. If you cannot
   identify whether someone is singing or speaking from the audio,
   say "performing" or "addressing the audience" rather than asserting
   the content type.

5. Audio-aware but visually grounded. You can hear the audio in the
   video. If you flag an audio issue (clipping, sudden cut, wind
   noise), say so plainly. Do not flag stylistic audio choices.

   Activity-naming guard: when a person on stage is holding a
   microphone, NEVER call them "the speaker" or describe what they
   say as a "spoken interaction" unless you can clearly distinguish
   spoken-word content from singing in the audio. Default to "the
   performer" / "the singer" / "performing" / "singing into the
   microphone". If the audio shifts from singing to speaking
   (or vice versa) at a specific moment, that is itself a useful
   structural observation — describe the shift, do not retroactively
   relabel one half as "spoken" and the other as "musical".

6. Colour names: use direct colour names for stage and event
   lighting — magenta, purple, green, orange, blue. Do NOT use
   "pink", "warm", "cool" for stage lighting; reserve warm/cool
   only for naturalistic light. This stays consistent with the
   description track on the same video.

7. Apply the same general style rules as the description-generation
   task: present tense, third person, no editorialising ("beautiful",
   "stunning", "poorly framed"), no narrative purpose inference ("the
   creator intends to...", "this establishes..."), no prefatory
   phrases ("the video shows...", "we can see...").

8. Do not duplicate observations — across segments OR across
   suggestions in the bank.
   - Cross-segment: if two segments share the same issue, write one
     suggestion that names both via the `relatedScene` array.
   - Within-bank: if two suggestions point at the same underlying
     property of the video (e.g. "monotonous framing" restated as a
     framing observation, a single-shot observation, and a
     no-close-ups observation), keep one and use the remaining bank
     slots for genuinely different observations. The bank should
     surface diverse editing openings, not the same opening rephrased
     multiple ways.

9. Distribute across categories. Aim for roughly:
     2 to 3 issue flags
     2 to 3 structural
     2 to 3 creative
   Total 6 to 8. Always include at least one of each. If the video
   genuinely lacks material in a category (e.g. no technical issues
   at all), produce only one item there and stretch the others.

================================================================
INPUT YOU WILL RECEIVE
================================================================

You will be given:
  - The full video file as a multimodal input.
  - A `segments` list with each segment's id, index (zero-based),
    label, start_seconds, and end_seconds. Use these for
    `relatedScene` references — the index field is what you cite.

Optional context the researcher may also pass through:
  - `creator_goals`: 2 to 3 free-text things the creator said they
    wanted to "change or get right" about this video. If present, let
    these guide your selection — favour suggestions that touch the
    creator's stated goals — but do not invent observations that
    aren't supported by what's in the video.

================================================================
OUTPUT FORMAT
================================================================

Respond with valid JSON only, matching this schema exactly. Do not
wrap in markdown code fences. Do not include preamble or explanation.

{
  "suggestions": [
    {
      "id": "sug_001",
      "category": "issue" | "structural" | "creative",
      "text": "string — one observational sentence, 12 to 30 words",
      "relatedScene": 0 | [0, 1] | etc.
    }
  ]
}

`id` values are zero-padded sequential strings (`sug_001`, `sug_002`,
…). `relatedScene` is either a single integer or an array of integers
referring to segment `index` values.

Produce 6 to 8 items. No more, no fewer.
